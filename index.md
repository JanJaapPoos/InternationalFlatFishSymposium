The International Flatfish Symposium is a long-running scientific conference dedicated to research on flatfish ecology, fisheries, aquaculture, and marine ecosystem dynamics. Over the years, the symposium has played an important role in shaping international collaboration in flatfish research. Meetings are intentionally kept relatively small to encourage discussion and active participation, and many contributions are later published in special issues of the Journal of Sea Research. The conference has been hosted in countries across Europe, North America, and Asia, reflecting the global importance of flatfish species for both ecosystems and fisheries. By fostering collaboration between researchers from different disciplines and regions, the International Flatfish Symposium continues to contribute to advances in sustainable fisheries management and marine conservation.

The next symposium will be held in 2028, in Sendai, Japan.

The concept of the flatfish symposia was initiated at the 1989 European Marine Biology Symposium in Oban, Scotland. A group of young scientists set up an international forum to discuss and exchange their ideas and results on fish ecology. An organizing committee was formed and in 1990 the first international symposium was held at the Royal Netherlands Institute for Sea Research (NIOZ). In the beginning, NIOZ played a central role by hosting the symposia in 1990, 1993 and 1996. The flatfish symposium 'left' NIOZ and subsequent editions were organized by research institutes and universities around the world. Due to COVID 19, the flatfish symposium 2020 was initially postponed and ultimately cancelled. A symposium was organized in 2024 in Wageningen, The Netherlands. 

The basic underpinning philosophy of the symposia has remained the same: it should be an informal international platform of high scientific quality for exchange of ideas and results and for the strengthening of international cooperation. To meet these scientific goals, the symposia should be challenging and the topics should have continuity over time (e.g. develop a research agenda). Thus far the objectives have been achievable: the topic of each sym posium was based on the results of the previous symposium. The number of participants has not been excessively high, in the order of 100-130 participants. This allowed presentations and discussions to be held in plenary, welcoming young scientists to contribute.

The symposium has traditionally been organized by two main committees: a Steering Committee and an Organizing Committee. The Steering Committee provides overall guidance, sets the symposium's goals and themes, and ensures that the scientific quality and relevance are maintained. The Organizing Committee is responsible for handling the logistical aspects of the symposium, including event planning, venue selection, registration, and scheduling sessions.
